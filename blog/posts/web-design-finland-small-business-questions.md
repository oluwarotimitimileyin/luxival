<h1>What Finnish Small Businesses Ask About Web Design — Answered</h1>

<div class="post-body">
  <p>Most small business owners in Finland know they need a better website. What they don't always know is what "better" actually means — and whether investing in web design will make a real difference to their revenue. I'll answer the questions I hear most directly and without jargon.</p>

  <h2>1. How Much Does a Website Cost in Finland?</h2>
  <p>A basic template website can cost €500–€2,000. A properly built custom website — designed for conversion, SEO, and your specific business — typically runs €3,000–€8,000 for a small business. That range reflects the difference between a site that looks like a website and a site that actually brings in customers. In my opinion, the more important question is: what does your current website cost you in lost business every month?</p>

  <h2>2. Do I Need a Finnish Web Designer?</h2>
  <p>Not necessarily — but you need someone who understands your market, your language requirements (Finnish, Swedish, and English), and the specific expectations of Finnish customers. Local knowledge matters for SEO, for tone, and for understanding what Finnish consumers trust. A Finnish designer will understand the nuances that an overseas agency simply won't.</p>

  <h2>3. How Long Does It Take to Build a Website?</h2>
  <p>A simple service website can be built and launched in 2–4 weeks. A larger e-commerce platform or a custom system with integrations typically takes 6–12 weeks. The biggest delay in most web projects is not the development — it's waiting for content, images, and decisions from the client. Come prepared with your copy, your visuals, and your priorities and your project will move fast.</p>

  <h2>4. Will a New Website Actually Bring More Customers?</h2>
  <p>A new website by itself won't bring customers — a well-built website that is also optimised for search engines and designed around conversion will. There's a difference between a website that exists and a website that works. The first just holds information. The second guides visitors toward taking action — booking, buying, calling, or enquiring.</p>

  <h2>5. What Is the Most Important Thing on a Business Website?</h2>
  <p>Clarity. The visitor should understand within three seconds what you do, who it's for, and what to do next. Most small business websites fail because they're trying to say too much at once. Choose one primary message. Have one primary call-to-action. Make both obvious the moment the page loads. Everything else is secondary.</p>

  <h2>6. Should My Website Be in Finnish or English?</h2>
  <p>Both, if you want to reach the widest audience. Finnish-speaking customers expect Finnish content — it builds trust. English opens you to international visitors, expats, and business travellers. For most service businesses in Helsinki, a bilingual site with Finnish as the primary language and English as a secondary option is the right approach.</p>
